Alameda County is a geographically diverse, densely populated county spanning across the East San Francisco Bay Area. 
The county lies between the Hayward and Calaveras fault lines, along with many minor fault lines in the area. 
In fact, most Bay Area residents reside within 10 miles of a fault line, putting the region at high risk for catastrophic earthquakes (earthquakecountry.org). 
This study aims to quantify the risk of earthquake damage, along with providing a measure of people living in high-risk zones in Alameda County. 
To accomplish this, we utilized the GIS multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) methodology presented in Jacek Malczewski’s GIS and Multicriteria Decision Analysis.  

The factors incorporated in the MCDA are vector data on liquefaction zones, vector data on fault lines in Alameda County, and census population density data obtained from data.sfgov.org, maps.conservation.ca.gov, and data.census.gov, respectively.
Locations of liquefaction zones were chosen as a factor due to the major risk posed to infrastructure, as damaged or collapsed buildings can significantly harm people living within these zones. 
Additionally, fault-line proximity was chosen as another factor because proximity can impact the magnitude felt, resulting in higher levels of infrastructure damage at high proximity. 
Finally, population density was chosen as a third factor to highlight the locations where buildings may be closer together, increasing the risk of collapsing buildings as well as fire danger. 
We also included population density to estimate the number of people living in high-risk census tracts, which can be used in collaboration with environmental policy planning agencies to measure risk reduction.
